# SkillsCake Marketing

Write marketing copy for SkillsCake that converts. This skill covers landing pages, ad hooks, articles, outreach emails, CTAs, and any public-facing copy across `skillscake-web` and `skillscake-product/marketing/`.

## The Product

SkillsCake makes AI agent skills better. A user uploads a skill file (or describes what they want), SkillsCake runs it through an improve→compare loop, and they get back a skill that actually works. Works for Cursor, Claude Code, VS Code, GitHub Copilot, Gemini CLI, ChatGPT, and dozens more. No CLI, no manual iteration, no lock-in.

- **Tagline:** Skills? Piece of cake.
- **Positioning:** Only drag-and-drop, zero-install, zero-data-risk skill optimizer. Context engineering, not prompt tweaking.
- **Pricing:** Pay-per-run. Base $3.20/run, Pro $6.00/run (25-pack). First run free. CC required at signup.
- **Company:** Agent Horizon LLC. Founded by Ryan Scheinberg.

## The Avatar

Developers and hobbyists who build or want to build AI agent skills.

### Primary segments (narrow — pick one per piece of copy)

- **Cursor power users** — use rules/skills daily, want them sharper
- **Claude Code builders** — shipping with agents, care about reliability
- **Indie hackers / solopreneurs** — using AI agents to run their business, need skills that don't embarrass them
- **Teams with agents in production** — want consistent, tested skills across their stack
- **Hobbyists / tinkerers** — excited about AI, want their first skill to actually work
- **Non-technical people** — know AI can help, don't know how to write good instructions for it

### What they say to themselves

- "My agent gives generic garbage unless I babysit it"
- "I wrote a prompt but it only works half the time"
- "I don't know what a good skill looks like"
- "I don't have time to iterate on prompts for hours"
- "Everyone says AI is amazing but mine keeps screwing up basic tasks"

Use their language. Not ours.

## How to Write SkillsCake Copy

### 1. Headlines and hooks are 80% of the job

Spend disproportionate effort on the first line. David Ogilvy: "When you've written your headline, you've spent 80 cents of your advertising dollar."

- The headline frames everything that follows — it changes how people interpret the rest
- A single headline change can 2-4x conversion without touching anything else
- Write 10-20 headline candidates before picking one
- Test: would the avatar stop scrolling for this? Would they say "this is for me"?

**Headline principles:**

- Clear beats clever. Deletion beats explanation.
- Run it through a reading-level checker — aim for third grade. Smart people appreciate simple language. 30% of people can barely read. Accessible ≠ dumb.
- Call out the avatar specifically: "Cursor users:", "If your agent gives generic answers:", "Building with Claude Code?"
- The more specific the call-out, the higher the conversion — even though it feels like you're shrinking the audience
- Start with a problem they'll nod at, a question they'll say yes to, or a direct address

**What works for SkillsCake headlines:**

- Problem-first: "Your agent is only as good as the skill behind it"
- Result-first: "0.14 to 0.79 in six minutes"
- Identity: "For people who build with AI agents"
- Contrast: "Same AI. Different skill."
- Specificity: "Your Cursor rules are the weakest part of your stack"

### 2. State the facts and tell the truth

Unsubstantiated claims destroy credibility with developers. Substantiated claims are wildly compelling.

- Use real numbers from real bakes
- If you can show a before/after, show it — don't describe it
- "We improved a lead-response skill from 0.62 to 0.81 in five minutes" is better than "We make your skills better"
- Track results → improve delivery → promote the stats → truthfully
- Fewer conditions on a claim = more compelling. "Average skill improves 40%+" beats "Skills with fewer than 500 words submitted as single files by users with prior prompt experience improve 40%+ when measured against..."

### 3. Show what only you can show

Demonstrations beat descriptions. SkillsCake has a structural advantage here: we produce before/after artifacts.

- **Link to real examples.** The comparison pages (`/example/*`) are the most powerful marketing assets we have.
- **Quote actual skill output.** Show the weak-skill response next to the strong-skill response.
- **Show the score delta.** 0.14 → 0.79 is visceral. People get it instantly.
- **Show the time.** "Six minutes" makes it real. They can picture doing it on a coffee break.
- Never say "we'll make it better" when you can show them what "better" looks like.

### 4. Give away the value, sell the convenience

The smart play: teach people what a good skill looks like for free. Let them try to do it themselves. The ones who can, respect you. The ones who can't, buy.

- The `/learn` page, articles, and content should be genuinely useful — not teasers
- "Make your free stuff better than their paid stuff"
- Lead magnets, articles, and content should fully solve narrow problems
- CTA should be about personalization, not "more" — "Want us to apply this to your skill?" not "Want more tips?"

### 5. Narrow and deep over broad and shallow

SkillsCake serves a niche. Lean into it.

- One piece of copy targets one segment of the avatar
- An ad for Cursor users shouldn't also try to reach ChatGPT hobbyists
- Deep specificity ("If you write .cursor/rules/ files") converts harder than broad appeals ("Make AI better")
- A page with 5,800 followers that speaks to exactly the right people outperforms one with 500k that speaks to everyone

### 6. Optimize front to back

Order of effort: headline → first paragraph → body → CTA.

You can 4x throughput by improving the headline. You might get 50% improvement optimizing the CTA. Put your time where the multiplier is.

**For landing pages:** Hero headline → sub-headline → first proof point. Everything below the fold matters less per pixel.

**For ads:** Hook (first 5 seconds / first line) → angle → CTA. Write 50 hooks, 3-5 body angles, 1-3 CTAs. 80% of hooks should be variations of what already worked. 20% experimental.

**For articles:** Title → subtitle → opening paragraph. If the title doesn't make them click, the brilliant section 4 doesn't exist.

### 7. Value equation framing

Every piece of copy should increase perceived value by addressing these four levers:

1. **Dream outcome** — what they actually want (agent that works reliably, not "optimized prompts")
2. **Perceived likelihood** — why they'll believe it works (scores, examples, testimonials, the fact that we show before/afters publicly)
3. **Speed** — how fast they get the result (~6 minutes, not days of iteration)
4. **Ease** — how little effort it takes (upload and walk away, no CLI, no manual tuning)

The ideal SkillsCake pitch hits all four: "Upload your skill, wait six minutes, get back one that actually works. Here's a real example."

## Copy Rules

- **Reading level:** Third grade. Always. Run it through a checker.
- **No jargon walls.** "Context engineering" is fine once if explained. Don't stack five technical terms in a paragraph.
- **No throat-clearing.** Cut "In today's rapidly evolving AI landscape..." — get to the point.
- **No weasel words.** Not "may help improve" — "improves." If you can't say it directly, the claim isn't strong enough.
- **Developer-aware but not developer-exclusive.** SkillsCake is for hobbyists too. Don't assume everyone knows what a YAML frontmatter block is.
- **Cake puns are allowed but not required.** "Piece of cake," "bake," "layers," "slices" are part of the brand vocabulary. Use them naturally, not forced.
- **Short paragraphs.** 1-3 sentences max. Web copy isn't an essay.
- **Active voice.** "SkillsCake tests your skill" not "Your skill is tested by SkillsCake."
- **No em dashes in shipped copy** (web UI, ads, emails). Do not use `—` or `--` as a dash. Use a period, comma, colon, or split into two short sentences.

## GEO (Generative Engine Optimization)

Content should be structured so AI search engines can extract and cite it:

- Use headers as natural language questions people actually ask
- Put the direct answer in the first paragraph — no buildup
- Specific claims with evidence over marketing prose
- Structured content (tables, numbered lists) that LLMs can parse

## Voice

SkillsCake copy should feel like a smart friend who's genuinely good at this stuff explaining it plainly. Not corporate. Not hype. Not academic.

- Confident but not arrogant
- Specific but not dense
- Casual but not sloppy
- Direct — say what you mean in as few words as possible
- Occasional dry humor is fine. Forced cleverness is not.

## Anti-patterns (Never Do These)

- "Unlock the power of AI" or any variant
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "Leverage cutting-edge technology to..."
- "Our proprietary algorithm..."
- Promising things we can't demonstrate with data
- Writing copy that could apply to any AI product — if you swap "SkillsCake" for "AnyAITool" and it still works, it's too generic
- Putting the CTA before proving the value
- Burying the proof below paragraphs of explanation
- Using screenshots of code as the primary visual when speaking to non-technical avatars

## Process When Writing New Copy

1. **Who is reading this?** Pick one avatar segment. Write for them.
2. **What's the one thing they should do after reading?** That's your CTA. Work backwards.
3. **Write 10+ headline candidates.** Pick the one that makes the avatar stop and say "this is for me."
4. **Lead with proof or a problem.** Not with a description of the product.
5. **Draft the body.** Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph.
6. **Cut 30%.** Then cut another 10%. If it still makes sense, you're close.
7. **Read it aloud.** If you stumble, rewrite that sentence.
8. **Check reading level.** Third grade. No exceptions.
9. **Final check:** Would a Cursor user who's never heard of SkillsCake understand what this is and why they should care within 10 seconds of landing?
